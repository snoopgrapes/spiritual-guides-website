Love Is Not a Feeling—It's a Daily Decision

An interdisciplinary study combining psychology, neuroscience, sociology, and theology

Introduction

The prevailing cultural narrative often portrays love as an uncontrollable emotion—a force that sweeps people off their feet, only to fade when the initial passion wanes. However, a growing body of research from psychology, neuroscience, sociology, and theology challenges this notion, presenting love as a conscious, deliberate choice rather than merely a fleeting feeling. This study synthesizes secular, academic, and biblical perspectives to argue that love is a daily commitment, requiring intentional action, sacrifice, and perseverance.

I. The Psychological Perspective: Love as a Choice

1. Cognitive and Behavioral Theories of Love

Psychological research distinguishes between:

  • Passionate love (intense emotional attraction)
  • Companionate love (deep friendship and commitment)
  • Compassionate love (selfless care for another's well-being)

Studies show that while initial attraction may be emotionally driven, long-term love depends on repeated decisions to prioritize the relationship.

Ecological Momentary Assessment (EMA) studies reveal that expressing love leads to feeling more loved, suggesting that love is reinforced through action. Behavioral Commitment Theory (Stanley & Markman) posits that love thrives when partners choose to invest in the relationship despite challenges.

2. The Role of Habit and Routine

Neuroplasticity research indicates that repeated loving behaviors (e.g., acts of kindness, active listening) strengthen neural pathways associated with attachment. "Positivity Resonance" theory (Fredrickson) argues that love is sustained through micro-moments of connection, not just grand gestures.

II. The Sociological and Relational Science Perspective

1. Covenant vs. Contractual Love

Covenant marriages (legally binding in some U.S. states) emphasize lifelong commitment, premarital counseling, and restricted divorce options. Research shows these marriages have lower divorce rates due to intentionality in sustaining love.

Secular vs. Religious Commitment: Studies find that couples with strong moral or religious commitments exhibit higher marital stability, as they view love as a sacred duty rather than an emotion. This aligns with biblical teachings like Malachi 2:14-16 and Ephesians 5:25-33.

2. The Impact of Individualism on Love

Modern culture's emphasis on personal happiness often undermines relational commitment. However, research shows that:

  • Selfishness is the #1 enemy of lasting love
  • Couples who frame love as a "team effort" (rather than individual fulfillment) report higher satisfaction

III. The Neuroscience of Love: Beyond Emotions

1. Love as a Motivational Drive (Not Just an Emotion)

Neurobiological studies compare romantic love to addiction, involving dopamine-driven cravings for connection. Unlike emotions (short-lived), love activates long-term attachment systems (oxytocin and vasopressin).

2. The "Love Withdrawal Syndrome" Hypothesis

Some researchers argue that love should be classified as a physiological drive (like hunger), not an emotion, because:

  • It persists even when feelings fade
  • It requires active maintenance (e.g., communication, forgiveness) as emphasized in Colossians 3:12-14

IV. The Biblical and Theological Perspective

1. Love as a Covenant (Not a Contract)

Biblical marriage is modeled on God's covenant love (Hosea 2:19-20, Ephesians 5:25), which is unconditional and sacrificial. 1 Corinthians 13:4-7 defines love as patient, kind, and enduring—qualities that require daily decisions, not just feelings.

2. The Role of Faith in Sustaining Love

Active religious couples (e.g., those who pray together) report higher marital commitment. Ecclesiastes 4:12 ("A threefold cord is not quickly broken") emphasizes that God-centered love is more resilient.

V. Practical Applications: How to Choose Love Daily

1. Behavioral Strategies

  • Express love even when you don't "feel" it (studies show this reinforces emotional bonds)
  • Practice gratitude and appreciation (e.g., verbal affirmations, small acts of service) as modeled in 1 Thessalonians 5:11

2. Relational Habits

  • Prioritize communication (avoiding criticism, defensiveness, contempt, stonewalling—per Gottman's research)
  • Schedule regular "check-ins" to discuss needs and conflicts

3. Spiritual Disciplines

  • Pray together (couples who do this have lower divorce rates)
  • View marriage as a reflection of Christ's love (selfless, forgiving, enduring) as in John 13:34-35

Conclusion: Love Is a Verb

Love is not a passive emotion but an active, daily decision—one supported by psychology, neuroscience, sociology, and Scripture. While feelings may fluctuate, commitment, intentionality, and sacrifice sustain love over time. Whether secular or religious, the most enduring relationships are those where partners choose love every day.

Key Takeaways:

  • Love is reinforced through action (expressing love increases felt love)
  • Commitment outweighs emotion in long-term relationships
  • Faith and shared purpose strengthen relational resilience
  • Neurobiology confirms love is a drive, not just a feeling
  • Biblical love is covenantal, demanding daily sacrifice as shown in Romans 5:8
